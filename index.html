You Didn't Choose to Keep Scrolling. Someone Designed It That Way.

The attention economy is not a metaphor. It's a system engineered to extract your focus, sell it for profit, and resist every attempt at regulation.

April 2026A Project in Public Writing

You open your phone to check one notification. Thirty minutes later you're still scrolling, and you're not entirely sure what happened in between. It feels like a personal failure, a lack of discipline, maybe, or a bad habit you should have outgrown by now. But that framing misses what's actually happening. The experience you just had wasn't an accident. It was the product working exactly as intended.

In 1971, the economist Herbert Simon made a prediction that turned out to be one of the most important observations of the twentieth century. In a world flooded with information, he argued, the scarce resource wouldn't be content. It would be the human attention needed to process it. Simon pointed out that people can only truly focus on one thing at a time, making attention a hard constraint. You can redirect it, but you can't expand it. More than fifty years later, the digital economy has confirmed Simon's warning and taken it to extremes he likely never imagined.

Today, platforms like TikTok, Instagram, YouTube, and Facebook don't just compete for your attention. They treat it as a raw material, something to be extracted, measured, and sold. Researchers Thomas Franke and colleagues describe what they call a "second wave" of attention economics, where human focus has become a fully interchangeable currency, traded not just in advertising markets but across everyday digital life. Your attention isn't a metaphor for money. It functions like money.

Part I

Engineered to Capture

So how does the system actually work? Not through some vague algorithmic magic, but through specific, well-documented design choices. A 2023 study published at the CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems reviewed forty-three prior studies and identified eleven distinct interface patterns that platforms use to hijack user attention. The researchers call these Attention Capture Damaging Patterns, and you already know most of them by feel, even if you've never heard the term.

These aren't bugs or oversights. They are features, built on research into cognitive biases and deployed at scale. Legal scholar Tim Wu calls the underlying business model "attention brokerage": companies offer you a free service, capture your attention while you use it, and then resell that attention along with your behavioral data to advertisers. Wu traces this model back over a century, from newspaper ads to radio to television to the modern internet. The basic mechanism has never changed. What's changed is how good the capture technology has become.

And the invisibility is deliberate. There's no loading bar that says "attention extracted." No notification that your focus just became someone else's revenue stream. From your side of the screen, everything feels free. That feeling, the sense that you're getting something for nothing, is the engine that makes the entire system run.

Part II

What It's Costing You

Gloria Mark, a professor of informatics at UC Irvine, has spent nearly twenty years measuring what digital environments do to human cognition. Her findings are sobering. The average time a person spends on a single screen before switching has dropped from about two and a half minutes in 2004 to just forty-seven seconds. That isn't just an inconvenience. Mark's research links constant task-switching to elevated stress hormones, higher error rates, and a progressive loss of the ability to sustain self-directed focus. The more your attention gets pulled in different directions, the harder it becomes to steer it yourself.

Philosopher James Williams, a former Google advertising strategist who became an ethicist at the Oxford Internet Institute, argues that these harms escalate through three distinct levels.

Functional Distraction You can't finish what you sat down to do. Tasks take longer, mistakes increase, and your day slips out from under you.
Existential Distraction Your daily activity starts drifting from the things you actually value. You spend time in ways that don't reflect who you want to be.
Epistemic Distraction You lose the ability to think clearly about what kind of life you even want. This is where democracy starts to erode.

That third level is where the stakes become political. Self-governance requires the capacity for sustained reflection, the ability to step back, weigh competing values, and make deliberate choices. That is exactly the capacity the attention economy degrades, not as a side effect, but as a structural consequence of its design.

Writer and Stanford educator Jenny Odell pushes this argument into the civic sphere. The attention economy, she contends, actively colonizes your inner life, crowding out the slow, place-based, reflective thought that democratic participation depends on. Odell argues that individual willpower can't counter a system designed at industrial scale. Instead, she calls for what she terms "refusal-in-place," a deliberate, communal redirection of attention toward local engagement and public life.

The harms also show up in public health data. A systematic review of fifty studies found consistent negative associations between social media use and adolescent mental health, with particularly elevated risks of depression among teenage girls. The researchers note the field needs better tools to separate what young people see online from the sheer volume of time spent, but the overall direction of the evidence is difficult to dismiss.

Underlying all of this is what Harvard scholar Shoshana Zuboff calls "surveillance capitalism," a system in which behavioral data is extracted from users, processed into prediction products, and sold in behavioral futures markets through mechanisms most users never see and never consent to. The picture that emerges is not a collection of isolated distractions. It is a coordinated system degrading autonomy from the cognitive level upward through the civic.

Part III

Why the Law Hasn't Caught Up

If the evidence is this clear, why hasn't the legal system acted? The short answer: the law was built for a different economy. Tim Wu argues that antitrust and consumer protection law contain a fundamental blind spot. Because platforms like Google and Facebook offer their services for free, regulators have struggled to identify consumer harm using frameworks built around price. If you didn't pay anything, the reasoning goes, you can't have been harmed.

Wu proposes a solution: an Attentional-SSNIP test, modeled on existing antitrust tools, that would measure harm not through price increases but through increases in advertising load. It's a step in the right direction, but political scientist Isabella Mariani argues it may not be enough. More competition among attention-extracting platforms, she points out, just gives users a choice between different instruments of capture. The real question isn't which platform wins the market. It's whether any of them should be permitted to operate this way.

Mariani points to the European Union's emerging framework, including the Digital Services Act, the Data Act, and the AI Act, as models that treat user autonomy as a value worth protecting directly, rather than hoping market competition will protect it by default. These laws include upfront restrictions on dark patterns and programmatic advertising, meaning they try to prevent harm before it happens rather than punish it afterward.

The attention economy is not a series of personal failures. It is a system: engineered at the design level, sustained by surveillance infrastructure, and operating in a legal vacuum that wasn't built to see it. Fixing it will require structural reform, regulation that constrains extraction before it starts, paired with a cultural reclamation of the capacity for sustained, self-directed thought. Neither alone is sufficient. But the research is clear, the harms are documented across psychology, law, informatics, philosophy, and political science, and the window for thoughtful action is still open.
